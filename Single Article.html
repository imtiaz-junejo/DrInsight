What Is a Migraine?

Migraine is a complex neurological disorder characterised by recurrent, moderate-to-severe headaches lasting between 4 and 72 hours. It is far more than a "bad headache" — it is the second most disabling neurological condition globally, affecting approximately 1 billion people worldwide.1

Unlike tension-type headaches, migraines involve distinct phases, neurological symptoms, and a pathophysiology rooted in cortical spreading depression and trigeminovascular activation. The condition affects three times more women than men, with peak prevalence between the ages of 25 and 55.2

🔬 Research Note

A 2023 Global Burden of Disease study ranked migraine as the leading cause of disability in people under 50 years old, surpassing stroke, dementia, and multiple sclerosis combined. (GBD 2023, Lancet Neurology)

Types of Migraine

The International Classification of Headache Disorders (ICHD-3) recognises several distinct migraine subtypes. The two most common are:

  • Migraine without aura (MO): Accounts for ~75% of cases. Pulsating unilateral headache with nausea, photo/phonophobia.
  • Migraine with aura (MA): Preceded by transient, reversible neurological symptoms — visual disturbances (zig-zag lines, scotoma), sensory changes, or speech difficulties — lasting 20–60 minutes.
  • Chronic migraine: ≥15 headache days/month for >3 months, of which ≥8 fulfil migraine criteria.
  • Hemiplegic migraine: Rare, familial or sporadic form with motor aura mimicking stroke.
  • Vestibular migraine: Associated with vertigo, balance disturbance; most common cause of episodic vertigo in adults.

Symptoms & The Four Phases

Migraines classically progress through four distinct phases, though not all patients experience every phase:

PhaseTimingKey Symptoms
Prodrome24–48h beforeMood changes, food cravings, yawning, neck stiffness, fatigue
Aura20–60 min before/duringVisual zigzags, scotoma, tingling, speech difficulty (in MA only)
Headache4–72 hoursUnilateral pulsating pain, nausea/vomiting, photo/phonophobia
PostdromeUp to 48h after"Migraine hangover" — fatigue, cognitive fog, mood change

Diagnosis: ICHD-3 Criteria

Migraine is a clinical diagnosis based on history and neurological examination. No biomarker or imaging finding is specific. Diagnosis of migraine without aura requires ≥5 attacks fulfilling ICHD-3 criteria (POUND mnemonic):

  1. Pulsatile quality
  2. One-day duration (4–72 hours)
  3. Unilateral location
  4. Nausea or vomiting
  5. Disabling intensity
⚠️ Red Flag Symptoms — Seek Emergency Care

Call 911 or go to ER immediately if headache is: "thunderclap" (worst ever, sudden onset) · associated with fever + neck stiffness · with focal neurological deficit lasting >60 min · in a patient with cancer/immunosuppression · or following head trauma. These warrant immediate CT/MRI to exclude subarachnoid haemorrhage, meningitis, or intracranial mass.

Common Migraine Triggers

Triggers do not cause migraines — they lower the threshold for attacks in susceptible individuals. Identifying and managing personal triggers can significantly reduce attack frequency.

CategoryCommon TriggersManagement Strategy
HormonalMenstruation, OCP changes, perimenopauseCycle mapping; consider mini-pill; discuss HRT with gynaecologist
SleepToo little OR too much sleep, jet lagConsistent sleep schedule; CBT-I for insomnia
DietaryAlcohol (esp. red wine), caffeine (excess/withdrawal), aged cheeses, MSGMigraine diary; caffeine tapering; elimination trial
SensoryBright lights, strong smells, loud noiseFL-41 tinted glasses; noise-cancelling headphones
PsychologicalStress, anxiety, depressionCBT, mindfulness, biofeedback; psychiatric co-management if needed
PhysicalDehydration, skipped meals, exercise (occasionally)Regular hydration, meal schedule; warm-up before exercise
✅ Clinical Tip

Keep a digital migraine diary for at least 3 months. Record date, duration, severity (0–10), possible triggers, medications used, and menstrual cycle. Apps like Migraine Buddy or N1-Headache are evidence-validated tools that generate reports printable for your neurologist.

Evidence-Based Treatment Options

Migraine management is divided into acute (abortive) treatment — taken during an attack — and preventive treatment taken daily to reduce attack frequency. Treatment should be stepped according to disability and response.

Acute Treatments

💊 Medication Note

All medications listed require a valid prescription and should be used only under medical supervision. Do not exceed recommended doses. Overuse of acute medications (>10–15 days/month) can cause Medication Overuse Headache (MOH), significantly worsening migraine frequency.

Drug ClassExamplesEvidence LevelNotes
Triptans (5-HT1B/1D agonists)Sumatriptan, Rizatriptan, Eletriptan⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ AFirst-line specific therapy. Most effective when taken early. Avoid in cardiovascular disease.
CGRP Antagonists (Gepants)Ubrogepant, Rimegepant, Zavegepant⭐⭐⭐⭐ ANewer class; fewer CV contraindications; also has preventive effect (rimegepant).
Ditans (5-HT1F agonists)Lasmiditan⭐⭐⭐⭐ ANo vasoconstriction; useful in CV-risk patients. Avoid driving 8h after dose.
NSAIDsIbuprofen, Naproxen, Aspirin⭐⭐⭐ BEffective for mild-moderate attacks. Combine with antiemetic for best effect.
AntiemeticsMetoclopramide, Prochlorperazine⭐⭐⭐ BImprove gastric absorption of analgesics; treat nausea independently.

Preventive Medications

Prevention is indicated when attacks occur ≥4 days/month, are severely disabling, or are unresponsive to acute treatments. Typically requires 3–6 months of treatment to assess efficacy.

  • Beta-blockers: Propranolol, Metoprolol — first-line; 50% reduction in attack frequency in ~50% of patients
  • Antiepileptics: Topiramate, Valproate — effective but require monitoring (teratogenic — avoid in pregnancy)
  • Tricyclics: Amitriptyline — especially useful with comorbid insomnia or depression
  • CGRP mAbs (Anti-CGRP): Erenumab (Aimovig), Fremanezumab, Galcanezumab — monthly/quarterly injections; highly effective for chronic migraine with minimal systemic side effects
  • OnabotulinumtoxinA (Botox): Approved for chronic migraine; 31-injection protocol every 12 weeks
📌 Key Takeaway

Anti-CGRP monoclonal antibodies represent the first migraine-specific preventive treatments ever developed, with phase III trials showing a 50%+ reduction in monthly migraine days in approximately 50–70% of patients. They are now recommended by NICE, AAN, and EHF as first or second-line preventive therapy.

When to See a Doctor

You should consult a neurologist or headache specialist if:

  • You experience ≥4 migraine days per month affecting quality of life
  • Over-the-counter medications are no longer effective
  • You are using acute medications more than 10 days per month
  • Your pattern of headaches has changed significantly
  • You have never received a formal migraine diagnosis

🎯 Key Takeaways

  • Migraine is a neurological disorder affecting 1 billion people — not simply a "bad headache"
  • Diagnosis is clinical, based on ICHD-3 criteria — no specific imaging or blood test exists
  • Triptans and CGRP gepants are first-line acute treatments; anti-CGRP mAbs are the most effective preventive option
  • Keeping a migraine diary is essential for identifying personal triggers and monitoring treatment response
  • Red flag features require immediate emergency evaluation — do not dismiss sudden severe headache

📖 Glossary of Medical Terms ▸
AuraTransient neurological symptoms (visual, sensory, or motor) that precede migraine headache; caused by cortical spreading depression
CGRPCalcitonin Gene-Related Peptide — a neuropeptide released during migraine attacks; key target of newer preventive medications
Cortical Spreading DepressionA wave of electrical silence moving across the brain cortex, believed to generate migraine aura
MOHMedication Overuse Headache — paradoxical worsening of headache from overuse of acute treatments (>10–15 days/month)
Monoclonal AntibodyLaboratory-made protein targeting specific molecules (like CGRP or its receptor); given by injection for migraine prevention
Trigeminovascular systemNeural pathway linking trigeminal nerve fibres with meningeal blood vessels; central to migraine pain transmission
